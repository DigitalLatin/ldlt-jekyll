               <tei-p xml:id="d2e22975" id="d2e22975" data-origname="p">This sentence describes the last and most impressive of the defensive measures
                  adopted by the Alexandrians against the newly arrived Caesar, namely, the
                  construction of moveable siege towers. <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e22977" id="d2e22977" data-origname="foreign">Obiectis</tei-foreign>
                  is the principal problem in this passage: its authenticity has long been
                  questioned, with <tei-ref target="bibliography-modern-editions.html#Scaliger">Scaliger</tei-ref> excising it and other
                  critics offering emendations. <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e22983" id="d2e22983" data-origname="foreign">Confixerant</tei-foreign>, too,
                  has been suspected.</tei-p>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e22988" id="d2e22988" data-origname="p">1) As transmitted, the verb used to report the towers’ construction is <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e22990" id="d2e22990" data-origname="foreign">confixerant</tei-foreign>, “had cobbled together.” At its one other
                  occurrence in the corpus in the context of construction the presence of an
                  instrumental ablative shows the verb to be used more literally: <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e22993" id="d2e22993" data-origname="hi">BG</tei-hi> 3.13.4 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e22996" id="d2e22996" data-origname="foreign">transtra ex pedalibus in altitudinem
                     trabibus confixa</tei-foreign> (<tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e22999" id="d2e22999" data-origname="hi">sc</tei-hi>. <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23002" id="d2e23002" data-origname="foreign">erant</tei-foreign>) <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23006" id="d2e23006" data-origname="foreign">clauis ferris</tei-foreign>.<tei-note place="foot" xml:id="d2e23009" id="d2e23009" data-origname="note">Similar usages are attested in other authors (<tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23011" id="d2e23011" data-origname="hi">TLL</tei-hi> 4.211.67–79 ‘figendo instruere’). Elsewhere in the corpus <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23014" id="d2e23014" data-origname="foreign">configere</tei-foreign> appears in the context of combat: <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23017" id="d2e23017" data-origname="hi">BAlex</tei-hi> 40.2 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23020" id="d2e23020" data-origname="foreign">legio … confixa et
                        oppressa est</tei-foreign>; <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23023" id="d2e23023" data-origname="hi">BAfr</tei-hi> 59.5 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23027" id="d2e23027" data-origname="foreign">exercitum … iaculis configeret.</tei-foreign></tei-note>
                  Construction-related passages lacking the ablative can be found in, e.g.,
                  Vitruvius (10.6.3 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23030" id="d2e23030" data-origname="foreign">tigna conlocantur in capitibus utraque
                     parte habentia transuersaria confixa</tei-foreign>; cf. 10.9.6, 10.15.7; see

                  <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23043" id="d2e23043" data-origname="foreign">quadriremes XXII, quinqueremes V confecerunt</tei-foreign>),

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e23090" id="d2e23090" data-origname="p">2) <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23092" id="d2e23092" data-origname="foreign">Obiectis</tei-foreign>, which occurs later in the
                  description of these moveable military towers, makes little sense in context: if
                  pack animals are pulling the towers, it is hard to see the relevance of their
                  having been “placed opposite,” “in front of,” or “in the way of” the towers. The
                  parallelism between <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23095" id="d2e23095" data-origname="foreign">funibus</tei-foreign> and <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23098" id="d2e23098" data-origname="foreign">iumentis</tei-foreign> is also peculiar.</tei-p>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e23102" id="d2e23102" data-origname="p"><tei-ref target="bibliography-modern-editions.html#Scaliger">Scaliger</tei-ref> addressed both problems by excising
                     <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23106" id="d2e23106" data-origname="foreign">obiectis</tei-foreign>, removing the reference to the
                  animals’ position and construing <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23109" id="d2e23109" data-origname="foreign">funibus
                     iumentisque</tei-foreign> as instrumental ablatives with <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23112" id="d2e23112" data-origname="foreign">mouebant</tei-foreign>. With this repair one can perhaps see <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23115" id="d2e23115" data-origname="foreign">obiectis</tei-foreign> as a garbled reprise of <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23118" id="d2e23118" data-origname="foreign">subiectis</tei-foreign> or a garbled anticipation of <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23122" id="d2e23122" data-origname="foreign">derectis</tei-foreign>. However, in the Caesarian corpus there is no other
                  instance of an instrumental ablative with <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23125" id="d2e23125" data-origname="foreign">moueo</tei-foreign>:
                  nobody ever moves an object by means of something else.<tei-note place="foot" xml:id="d2e23128" id="d2e23128" data-origname="note">The
                     verb <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23130" id="d2e23130" data-origname="foreign">moueo</tei-foreign> in Caesar is most often used in the
                     idiom <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23133" id="d2e23133" data-origname="foreign">mouere castra</tei-foreign> (<tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23136" id="d2e23136" data-origname="hi">BG</tei-hi>
                     1.39.7, 2.2.6, etc.) or in similar phrases involving the movement of troops or
                     the changing of location (<tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23139" id="d2e23139" data-origname="hi">BG</tei-hi> 3.15.3, etc.). Often
                        <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23142" id="d2e23142" data-origname="foreign">moueo</tei-foreign> is used in the passive voice, when
                     someone is figuratively moved by something <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23146" id="d2e23146" data-origname="hi">(BG</tei-hi> 7.76.2,
                        <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23149" id="d2e23149" data-origname="hi">BC</tei-hi> 1.4.2, etc.).</tei-note> This is a usage with few
                  parallels in Latin more generally (see <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23153" id="d2e23153" data-origname="hi">TLL</tei-hi> 8.1540.47–60).
                  In the passive <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23156" id="d2e23156" data-origname="foreign">moueo</tei-foreign> can be used with an ablative
                  of manner (cf. Cic. <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23159" id="d2e23159" data-origname="hi">Phil.</tei-hi> 9.14, something moved <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23163" id="d2e23163" data-origname="foreign">nulla ui</tei-foreign>) or absolutely, as when towers are moved
                  forward in sieges (cf. <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23166" id="d2e23166" data-origname="hi">BG</tei-hi> 2.31.1, Curt. 4.6.9). The best
                  parallel for an active <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23169" id="d2e23169" data-origname="foreign">moueo</tei-foreign> with an object and
                  an instrumental ablative — also a rope, as it turns out — comes from Propertius
                  (4.11.51 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23172" id="d2e23172" data-origname="foreign">tu, quae tardam mouisti fune Cybeben</tei-foreign>).
                  Because of the absence of relevant parallels for this usage, it seems prudent to
                  proceed with the assumption that a participle is needed where the text reads
                     <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23175" id="d2e23175" data-origname="foreign">obiectis</tei-foreign> in order to form a second ablative
                  absolute with <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23178" id="d2e23178" data-origname="foreign">funibus iumentisque</tei-foreign>.<tei-note place="foot" xml:id="d2e23182" id="d2e23182" data-origname="note">There is a comparable asyndetic pair of absolutes in a similar
                     string of different sorts of ablatives at <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23184" id="d2e23184" data-origname="hi">BAlex</tei-hi> 11.6,
                     which is also, incidentally, a passage about towing: <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23187" id="d2e23187" data-origname="foreign">Hac
                        calamitate perterritis hostibus aduerso uento leniter flante naues onerarias
                        Caesar remulco uictricibus suis nauibus Alexandriam deducit</tei-foreign>. The
                     expression <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23190" id="d2e23190" data-origname="foreign">naues remulco uictricibus suis nauibus
                        deducit</tei-foreign> is a decent structural parallel for <tei-ref target="bibliography-modern-editions.html#Scaliger">Scaliger</tei-ref>’s hypothesized <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23196" id="d2e23196" data-origname="foreign">eas
                        … funibus iumentisque … mouebant</tei-foreign>, but both <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23200" id="d2e23200" data-origname="foreign">remulco</tei-foreign> and <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23203" id="d2e23203" data-origname="foreign">nauibus</tei-foreign> work
                     reasonably well with <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23206" id="d2e23206" data-origname="foreign">deducit</tei-foreign> (cf. <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23209" id="d2e23209" data-origname="hi">BC</tei-hi> 3.40.1), whereas neither of our ablatives works well
                     with <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23212" id="d2e23212" data-origname="foreign">mouebant</tei-foreign>. For other long strings of
                     ablatives see 27.5 and 32.3.</tei-note></tei-p>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e23216" id="d2e23216" data-origname="p"><tei-ref target="bibliography-secondary-sources.html#Castiglioni">Castiglioni</tei-ref> emended <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23220" id="d2e23220" data-origname="foreign">obiectis</tei-foreign> to <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23223" id="d2e23223" data-origname="foreign">adiectis</tei-foreign>, assuming a
                  palaeographically plausible innovation. This gives the sense of “fastened upon” or
                  “added to.” Forms of <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23226" id="d2e23226" data-origname="foreign">adicere</tei-foreign> occur ten times in
                  the corpus, but it usually means to add on or adjoin something abstract or to
                  describe geographical space (cf., e.g., 28.3 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23229" id="d2e23229" data-origname="foreign">unum
                     latus</tei-foreign> [<tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23232" id="d2e23232" data-origname="hi">sc</tei-hi>. <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23236" id="d2e23236" data-origname="foreign">castrorum</tei-foreign>] <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23239" id="d2e23239" data-origname="foreign">erat adiectum flumini
                     Nilo</tei-foreign>). Here it would imply that the animals are added on to a larger
                  contraption, which does not yield perfect sense.</tei-p>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e23243" id="d2e23243" data-origname="p"><tei-ref target="bibliography-modern-editions.html#Klotz">Klotz</tei-ref> proposed <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23247" id="d2e23247" data-origname="foreign">obtectis</tei-foreign> to describe the animals, which would be “covered” or
                  “protected.” Caesar uses <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23250" id="d2e23250" data-origname="foreign">obtectus</tei-foreign> to refer to
                  military protection, which would suggest that the pack animals are shielded from
                  opposing attack (cf. <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23253" id="d2e23253" data-origname="hi">BC</tei-hi> 3.19.7 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23256" id="d2e23256" data-origname="foreign">quae
                        [<tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23258" id="d2e23258" data-origname="hi">sc</tei-hi>. tela] ille obtectus armis militum
                     uitauit</tei-foreign> and 3.54.1 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23262" id="d2e23262" data-origname="foreign">uineis eam partem castrorum
                     obtexit</tei-foreign>). The context of our sentence does not necessitate this idea,
                  and the necessity of taking <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23266" id="d2e23266" data-origname="foreign">iumentis</tei-foreign> in tandem
                  with <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23269" id="d2e23269" data-origname="foreign">funibus</tei-foreign> makes it unlikely.</tei-p>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e23273" id="d2e23273" data-origname="p">Contexts involving pack animals towing something frequently contain the verb
                     <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23275" id="d2e23275" data-origname="foreign">iungere</tei-foreign>. There are two potential problems with
                  an emendation based on <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23278" id="d2e23278" data-origname="foreign">iungere</tei-foreign>, however. First,
                     <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23281" id="d2e23281" data-origname="foreign">iunctis</tei-foreign> is paleographically further removed
                  from <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23284" id="d2e23284" data-origname="foreign">obiectis</tei-foreign> than either <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23287" id="d2e23287" data-origname="foreign">adiectis</tei-foreign> or <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23291" id="d2e23291" data-origname="foreign">obtectis</tei-foreign>. Second,
                     <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23294" id="d2e23294" data-origname="foreign">iungere</tei-foreign> usually refers to joining animals to
                  one another rather than to other objects. However, the verb does occur with the
                  etymologically related <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23297" id="d2e23297" data-origname="foreign">iumentum</tei-foreign> (Nepos <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23300" id="d2e23300" data-origname="hi">Timol.</tei-hi> 4.2, Varro <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23303" id="d2e23303" data-origname="hi">RR</tei-hi> 1.52.1; cf. Var.
                     <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23306" id="d2e23306" data-origname="hi">LL</tei-hi> 5.136) and in one passage of Vitruvius a yoked team
                  of oxen pull a rope (Vitr. 10.2.14 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23310" id="d2e23310" data-origname="foreign">bubus iunctis funem
                     ducebant [<tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23312" id="d2e23312" data-origname="hi">sc</tei-hi>. boues]</tei-foreign>). So <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23316" id="d2e23316" data-origname="foreign">iunctis</tei-foreign> is worth consideration here, even if <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23319" id="d2e23319" data-origname="foreign">funibus</tei-foreign> is a loose end.</tei-p>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e23324" id="d2e23324" data-origname="p">Compounds of <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23326" id="d2e23326" data-origname="foreign">iunctis</tei-foreign> are also worth considering.
                     <tei-ref target="bibliography-secondary-sources.html#Cornelissen">Cornelissen</tei-ref> suggested <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23332" id="d2e23332" data-origname="foreign">subiunctis</tei-foreign>, making the ropes and pack animals “subjoined” to the
                  towers. He cites as parallels a line from Columella in which bullocks are attached
                  to a plow (Col. 6.2.8 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23335" id="d2e23335" data-origname="foreign">uacuo plostro subiungendi</tei-foreign>)
                  and a line from Virgil in which tigers are joined to a chariot (Virg. <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23338" id="d2e23338" data-origname="hi">Ecl.</tei-hi> 5.29 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23342" id="d2e23342" data-origname="foreign">curru subiungere
                     tigres</tei-foreign>). Neither passage offers a parallel for <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23345" id="d2e23345" data-origname="foreign">funibus</tei-foreign>, which sits oddly with the prefix <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23348" id="d2e23348" data-origname="foreign">sub</tei-foreign>-, and the verb itself appears nowhere else in the corpus. The
                  form <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23351" id="d2e23351" data-origname="foreign">adiunctis</tei-foreign>, from a verb used eight times in
                  the <tei-title xml:id="d2e23354" id="d2e23354" data-origname="title">Bellum Alexandrinum</tei-title> and twenty-five times in the corpus
                  overall, is perhaps a better repair. Aulus Gellius uses <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23356" id="d2e23356" data-origname="foreign">adiunctis</tei-foreign> absolutely with <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23360" id="d2e23360" data-origname="foreign">iumenta</tei-foreign>
                  (Gel. 20.1.28 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23363" id="d2e23363" data-origname="foreign">uectaculum … quod adiunctis pecoribus
                     trahebatur</tei-foreign>), and Horace uses it jokingly to describe yoking mice to a
                  cart (Hor. <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e23366" id="d2e23366" data-origname="hi">Sat</tei-hi>. 2.3.247 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23369" id="d2e23369" data-origname="foreign">plostello
                     adiungere mures</tei-foreign>). However, nowhere in the corpus does <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23372" id="d2e23372" data-origname="foreign">adiungere</tei-foreign> describe affixing something to an object.</tei-p>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e23376" id="d2e23376" data-origname="p">It therefore seems best to leave <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e23378" id="d2e23378" data-origname="foreign">obiectis</tei-foreign> in its
                  imperfection with the understanding that the syntax and general sense are clear,
                  even if the original verb is not.</tei-p>
