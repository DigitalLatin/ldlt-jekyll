               <tei-p xml:id="d2e28955" id="d2e28955" data-origname="p">In support of his emendation he cited <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e28957" id="d2e28957" data-origname="hi">BC</tei-hi> 1.1.3 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e28960" id="d2e28960" data-origname="foreign">sin Caesarem respiciant atque eius gratiam sequantur</tei-foreign> as

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e28997" id="d2e28997" data-origname="p"><tei-ref target="bibliography-secondary-sources.html#Landgraf1891b">Landgraf</tei-ref> (1891b, 12–13) developed <tei-ref target="bibliography-secondary-sources.html#Larsen">Larsen</tei-ref>’s emendation in two ways:</tei-p>

               <tei-quote rend="blockquote" xml:id="d2e29005" id="d2e29005" data-origname="quote">cuius itineris duas est consecutus opportunitates </tei-quote>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e29008" id="d2e29008" data-origname="p">For the main verb he substituted one better paralleled in the <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e29010" id="d2e29010" data-origname="hi">corpus Caesarianum</tei-hi>, and he changed <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29013" id="d2e29013" data-origname="foreign">has</tei-foreign>
                  to <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29016" id="d2e29016" data-origname="foreign">duas</tei-foreign>, citing in support <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e29019" id="d2e29019" data-origname="hi">BC</tei-hi> 1.39.4: <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29022" id="d2e29022" data-origname="foreign">Quo facto duas res consecutus
                     est</tei-foreign>, a particularly apt parallel since it too is followed by two
                  indicative <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29026" id="d2e29026" data-origname="foreign">quod</tei-foreign> clauses.<tei-note place="foot" xml:id="d2e29029" id="d2e29029" data-origname="note"><tei-ref target="bibliography-modern-editions.html#Andrieu">Andrieu</tei-ref> 2002 [1954] ad loc. incorrectly reports <tei-ref target=".html#Landgraf">Landgraf</tei-ref>’s proposal as <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29036" id="d2e29036" data-origname="foreign">has
                        est consecutus</tei-foreign>
                     <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29039" id="d2e29039" data-origname="foreign">opportunitates</tei-foreign>.</tei-note></tei-p>

               <tei-p xml:id="d2e29043" id="d2e29043" data-origname="p">But <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29045" id="d2e29045" data-origname="foreign">certas</tei-foreign> does not seem indefensible. The
                  adjective is fairly common in the <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e29048" id="d2e29048" data-origname="hi">corpus Caesarianum</tei-hi>,

                  modifies a wide range of nouns: winds, places, routes, people, military units,
                  news, days, quantities, times, military formations, etc. It modifies hoped-for

                     condicio</tei-foreign>, where <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29064" id="d2e29064" data-origname="foreign">receptus</tei-foreign> and

                        uictoriam</tei-foreign>. Similarly future-oriented abstractions appear in
                     negative formulations such as <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e29081" id="d2e29081" data-origname="hi">BG</tei-hi> 5.29.5 <tei-hi rend="italic" xml:id="d2e29084" id="d2e29084" data-origname="hi">quis hoc sibi persuaderet sine certa spe Ambiorigem ad eius
                        modi consilium descendisse</tei-hi>, and Hirt. 8.32.1 <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29087" id="d2e29087" data-origname="foreign">nec
                        se sine certa pernicie … putarent prouinciae fines intrare
                     posse</tei-foreign>.</tei-note>

                  <tei-foreign xml:lang="la" xml:id="d2e29127" id="d2e29127" data-origname="foreign">poterat</tei-foreign>): such an attack might be unlikely, but
